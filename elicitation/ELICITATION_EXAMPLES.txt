================================================================================
ELICITATION — concrete prompt examples for the causal-mimicry pilot
================================================================================
Format: THREE-WAY pairwise causal direction query.
Source: Long et al. 2024 (LLMs as Effective Priors for Causal Graph Discovery);
        Susanti & Farber 2025 (data-driven CD with LLMs). Both use the 3-option
        {A->B, B->A, neither} structure with context RESET per query.

WHY THREE-WAY (not binary):
  - A causes B            (direction 1)
  - B causes A            (direction 2)
  - no causal relationship (correlation, or independence — has NO direction)
  Correlation having no direction is exactly why we need the third option, and
  it's what enables the DISCRIMINATION sub-score (causal vs. merely correlated).

PROTOCOL:
  - Reset context for every query (stateless) — blocks choice-supportive bias.
  - Sample k times per edge (e.g. k=20) -> a distribution over A/B/C, not a point.
  - On an OPEN model: read the A/B/C token probabilities directly.
  - Optionally also ask for verbalised confidence (Tian "just ask") for the
    calibration signal.

================================================================================
TEMPLATE (token-probability version — preferred on open models)
================================================================================
Among these three options, which one is most likely true:

(A) {subject} causes {object}
(B) {object} causes {subject}
(C) no causal relationship exists between {subject} and {object}

The answer is:

   -> read P(A), P(B), P(C) from the next-token distribution.

================================================================================
TEMPLATE (verbalised version — if you also want stated confidence)
================================================================================
Consider "{subject}" and "{object}".
First briefly consider each possibility, then give your answer.
Which is most likely:
(A) {subject} causes {object}
(B) {object} causes {subject}
(C) there is no causal relationship between them
Answer with the letter and your confidence (0-100%).

================================================================================
WORKED EXAMPLES
================================================================================

--- Example 1 : HIGH-frequency, genuinely causal (true = A) ---
Among these three options, which one is most likely true:
(A) smoking causes lung cancer
(B) lung cancer causes smoking
(C) no causal relationship exists between smoking and lung cancer
The answer is:

  ground truth: true_direction = subject->object (A),  is_causal = true
  scored in: DIRECTION sub-score (expect P(A) high if mimicry holds)
            + DISCRIMINATION (did it pick a causal option over C? yes = correct)

--- Example 2 : correlational, NOT causal (true = C) ---
Among these three options, which one is most likely true:
(A) rooster crowing causes sunrise
(B) sunrise causes rooster crowing
(C) no causal relationship exists between rooster crowing and sunrise
The answer is:

  ground truth: true_direction = none,  is_causal = false
  NOTE: (B) is the real-world causal story (light triggers crowing), but for the
        purpose of "is the *stated pair* causal," this item is designed as the
        correlational/none test. Decide per item what C means and label it in
        relations.json — be consistent.
  scored in: DISCRIMINATION sub-score (correct answer = C; asserting A or B = the
            'confidently invents causation' failure). NOT in direction sub-score
            (no true direction to get right).

--- Example 3 : LOW-frequency / abstract, genuinely causal (true = A) ---
Among these three options, which one is most likely true:
(A) {abstract_subject} causes {abstract_object}
(B) {abstract_object} causes {abstract_subject}
(C) no causal relationship exists between them
The answer is:

  ground truth: true_direction = subject->object (A),  is_causal = true
  PURPOSE: this is where mimicry should COLLAPSE if frequency drives it — low
           co-occurrence -> expect P(A) near chance (~0.33). The contrast with
           Example 1 is the whole experiment.

================================================================================
HOW EACH ANSWER FEEDS THE SCORE
================================================================================
For each edge, from the k samples you get P(A), P(B), P(C) (or counts).

DIRECTION sub-score (is_causal=true only):
   observed = mean P(correct direction token)        [A or B per ground truth]
   chance   = 1/3
   normalized = (observed - 1/3) / (1 - 1/3)

DISCRIMINATION sub-score (all edges):
   correct = (picked a causal option when is_causal=true)
             OR (picked C when is_causal=false)
   observed = fraction correct
   chance   = 1/2
   normalized = (observed - 1/2) / (1 - 1/2)

Combine across a graph's edges with the MEAN (size-invariant). Never the product.
